Leadership

This week at EDA we were asked to read Chapter 8 of 'Search Inside Yourself' by Chade-Meng Tan. I got to learn more about leading with compassion, influencing with goodness and communicating with insight.

Compassionate Leadership

Compassion is seen as a key enabler for encouraging the highest level of happiness.

Throughout my years of working in the commercial IT industry, I can give plenty of examples where managers weren't being as compassionate as what they could have been. The end product of staff being felt under-valued and eventually, leaving the organisation.

The SCARF Model

The SCARF model identifies the five domains of social experience that the brain perceives as primary rewards or threats. They are:

  1. Status - is about one's seniority or relative importance. It can feel threatened quite easily by colleagues giving you "feedback", or rewarded when you improve a certain skill.
  2. Certainty - is great for the brain. In times of uncertainty, the brain reacts in a different way, taking up valuable resources.
  3. Autonomy - is the perception of exerting control over one's environment. Often it's not the stress itself that is debilitating, but the feeling of helplessness in the face of stress.
  4. Relatedness - is the perception of of whether another person is a "friend" or "foe".
  5. Fairness - is about sacrificing one's own self-interest for the greater good.

Phase 0 has certaintly been interesting and given me many different obstacles and challenges to overcome. I have found the instruction material clear and precise which has given me certanty over what is required for sprints ahead.

I suspect "Relatedness" will come in handy during Phase 1, as we are really going to have to work as a team to move forward from week to week.

A Difficult Conversation

Difficult conversations are conversations that are often important, but hard to have. There are 5 steps to conduct a difficult conversation. They are:

  1. Prepare by walking through the "three conversations", e.g.
    • What happened?
    • What emotions are involved? and;
    • What does this say about me?
  2. Decide whether or not to raise the issue
  3. Start from the objective "third story"
  4. Explore their story and yours
  5. Problem solve

I can honestly say that I don't follow all these steps when navigating a difficult conversation, however moving forward these will come in handy.
